# Technical Writing

Use this skill when a task needs explanation, documentation, rewrite, summary, or a clearer presentation of technical material.

## Writing Goals

- Make the reader understand the system faster.
- Keep the structure obvious.
- Prefer concrete nouns and explicit transitions.
- Reduce cognitive load without deleting important detail.
- Preserve accuracy before style.

## Default Structure

Use this sequence unless the task clearly needs something else:

1. Short answer or executive summary
2. What the thing is
3. How it works
4. Why it is designed that way
5. Practical implications
6. Caveats or limitations

## Style Rules

- Use plain English first, jargon second.
- Define acronyms on first use.
- Prefer short paragraphs over long walls of text.
- Use headings to separate conceptual layers.
- Keep examples close to the claim they support.
- Avoid vague phrases like "simply", "obviously", or "basically" unless you are deliberately contrasting levels of detail.

## Editing Checklist

- Is the main point visible in the first few lines?
- Does each section have a single purpose?
- Are terms used consistently?
- Are the causal links explicit?
- Is the difference between canonical state and derived output clear?
- Is the reader told what to do next when action is expected?

## When Rewriting Documentation

- Preserve domain-specific meaning.
- Remove repetition that does not add a new idea.
- Convert hidden assumptions into explicit statements.
- Replace long, abstract descriptions with steps or examples when helpful.
- Use diagrams only when the flow is hard to follow in prose.

## When Summarizing

- Keep the summary faithful to the source.
- Do not invent conclusions that are not supported.
- Mention tradeoffs, constraints, and open questions when they matter.
- Distinguish facts from recommendations.

## Tone Guidance

- Be direct.
- Be calm.
- Be specific.
- Avoid hype.
- Write like a careful engineer explaining a system to another careful engineer.

## Output Checklist

- The reader should know what the topic is.
- The reader should know why it matters.
- The reader should know how it fits into the larger flow.
- The reader should know the next action or decision point.
